# Cold Email Writing

Expert cold email writer. Write emails that sound like they came from a sharp, thoughtful human — not a sales machine following a template.

## When to Use

- Writing outbound prospecting emails or cold follow-up sequences
- Getting replies from people with no existing relationship
- Sharpening subject lines, openings, CTAs, or personalization

## Before Writing

Check `.agents/product-marketing-context.md` first if it exists.

Understand the situation:

1. **Who:** Role, company, why this person specifically
2. **Goal:** Outcome you want (meeting, reply, intro, demo)
3. **Value:** Specific problem you solve for people like them
4. **Proof:** A result, case study, or credibility signal
5. **Signal:** Funding, hiring, LinkedIn posts, company news, tech changes

Work with whatever is provided — don't block on missing inputs.

## Writing Principles

**Write like a peer, not a vendor.** The email should read like it came from someone who understands their world. Use contractions. Read it aloud. If it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite it.

**Every sentence must earn its place.** Cold email is ruthlessly short. If a sentence doesn't move toward a reply, cut it.

**Personalization must connect to the problem.** If you remove the personalized opening and the email still makes sense, the personalization isn't working.

**Lead with their world, not yours.** "You/your" dominates over "I/we". Never open with who you are or what your company does.

**One ask, low friction.** "Worth exploring?" beats "Book a 30-minute call." One CTA per email.

## Common Structures

- **Observation → Problem → Proof → Ask:** You noticed X, which usually means Y challenge. We helped Z with that. Interested?
- **Question → Value → Ask:** Struggling with X? We do Y. Company Z saw [result]. Worth a look?
- **Trigger → Insight → Ask:** Congrats on X. That usually creates Y challenge. We've helped similar companies. Curious?

## Subject Lines

Short, boring, internal-looking. The subject line's only job is to get the email opened.

- 2-4 words, lowercase, no punctuation tricks
- Looks like it came from a colleague: "reply rates," "hiring ops," "Q2 forecast"
- No product pitches, no urgency, no emojis, no prospect's first name

## Voice & Tone

**Target voice:** Smart colleague who noticed something relevant.

**Calibrate by audience:**

- C-suite: ultra-brief, peer-level, understated
- Mid-level: more specific value, slightly more detail
- Technical: precise, no fluff, respect their intelligence

## What to Avoid

- Opening with "I hope this email finds you well" or "My name is X and I work at Y"
- Jargon: "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "best-in-class"
- Feature dumps (one proof point beats ten features)
- HTML, images, or multiple links
- Fake "Re:" or "Fwd:" subject lines
- Identical templates with only {{FirstName}} swapped
- Asking for 30-minute calls in first touch
- "Just checking in" follow-ups

## Follow-Up Sequences

Each follow-up adds something new — a different angle, fresh proof, a useful resource.

| Touch | Gap | Angle |
|-------|-----|-------|
| Email 1 | Day 0 | Main value prop |
| Email 2 | Day 3 | Different angle or proof |
| Email 3 | Day 7 | Useful resource or insight |
| Email 4 | Day 14 | Last attempt / breakup email |

**Breakup email:** Honor it — if you say it's your last email, make it your last email.

## Quality Check

Before presenting:

- Does it sound like a human wrote it? (Read aloud)
- Would YOU reply to this if you received it?
- Does every sentence serve the reader, not the sender?
- Is the personalization connected to the problem?
- Is there one clear, low-friction ask?
